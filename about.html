About Overlapse

Overlapse pairs archival photography with contemporary views to show how human decisions reshape our shared spaces. We invite residents, visitors, and planners to see the slow-motion story of our city unfold.

Our Mission

We believe that cities are living archives. Streets, riverfronts, and neighbourhood blocks are always evolving, yet their transformations are rarely visible in the moment. Overlapse exists to surface those hidden chapters and make the urban past tangible.

By presenting carefully matched before-and-after images, amplified with local context, we encourage thoughtful conversations about preservation, progress, and the people at the centre of both.

How We Build Each Story

  1. We collaborate with museums, libraries, and community historians to digitise and catalogue photographs that capture pivotal moments in the region.
  2. We revisit each site to recreate the perspective with present-day photography, respecting composition while embracing today's character.
  3. We pair the images in the Overlapse viewer, layering timelines, memories, and local voices to illuminate how the city has changed and what might come next.
"Overlapse helps us see that every skyline starts as a sketch, every plaza as a shared ambition. When you slide between moments, you witness the choices that build a city."

What You'll Discover

Urban Awareness

Trace the impact of planning decisions, infrastructure projects, and community advocacy across decades.

Local Memory

Follow stories from long-time residents and neighbourhood historians who lend context to every photograph.

Future Insight

Use the past to spark new ideas for climate resilience, housing, mobility, and the public realm.

Who's Behind Overlapse?

Overlapse is led by a collective of photographers, urbanists, designers, and civic storytellers. We work with municipal archives, universities, and community groups to surface narratives that are inclusive and representative.

Every new chapter begins with a listening session, gathering memories on porches, in libraries, and at local meetups to ensure the project reflects lived experience.
